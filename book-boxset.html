Book Boxset

Hand Lettering

Problem

A skill I wanted to feature for myself was hand lettering. It's a relevant skill to have, and one that I don't see many embracing or implementing beyond aesthetics. It's trendy, but it can be appropriate under the right circumstances.

Solution

I chose to apply this skill in the form of book covers. Through this medium, I show the relevance of lettering in the field of design. I chose classic book titles from Penguin Books, to re-imagine in this style.

Process

My process for this problem was a little different. I knew going into it I couldn't portray the same feel with each cover; the hand lettering would need to suit each individual story. I wanted to capture a timeless, vintage aesthetic through the color palette and simple patterning. My goal was to capture the classic era while maintaining a certain cohesiveness, but allow each cover to have its own lettering style, suited to the story.

I wanted the hand lettering to be the focal point of each cover. I wanted to continue with the hand-drawn theme, so I combined sketches with vector renderings to create seamless patterns as the imagery. Books included: Black Beauty, The Secret Garden, and Don Quixote.

For Black Beauty, I wanted to show contrast between beauty and wildness; I especially wanted to turn this on its head by doing "Black" in the script instead of "Beauty". For The Secret Garden, I aimed for a feeling of the wild garden before being tamed; overgrown and tangled. For Don Quixote, I gave a mild nod to the time period of the story, with a tight configuration that is reminiscent of illuminated manuscripts.

Knowing I wanted to use patterning as a background element that was textural in nature, I kept the lettering simple. I lettered in bold, thick strokes so that the titles would stand out from the patterns without competing.

Result

The final covers combined hand-lettered titles with illustrative patterns, each maintaining its own individuality while working as a cohesive set. I chose to keep the color schemes monochromatic. This helped tie them together as a set, while each maintained its own individuality.

I designed these covers as a set for Penguin Books, who publish many of classics and special edition collections. The final piece was a set of collectable bookmarks to accompany each book in the boxset. Mimicking the individual covers, each contains a hand-lettered quote from the stories within.
